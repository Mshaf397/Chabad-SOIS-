The Mitzvot of Yom Kippur

Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, is the holiest day of the Jewish year. It is a day of intense reflection, repentance, and prayer. Several mitzvot (commandments) are observed to honor the solemnity and sanctity of this day:

1. Fasting

The primary mitzvah of Yom Kippur is to fast for 25 hours, abstaining from food and drink. This fast is a powerful act of self-denial, focusing on spiritual rather than physical needs.

2. Refraining from Physical Comforts

In addition to fasting, five forms of physical pleasure are prohibited on Yom Kippur: eating and drinking, washing, applying lotions or oils, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations.

3. Prayer and Confession

Yom Kippur is characterized by extended synagogue services, including the recitation of the Vidui (confession) and the powerful Neilah prayer, which closes the day.

4. Repentance and Forgiveness

Before Yom Kippur begins, it is customary to seek forgiveness from others, acknowledging wrongdoings and asking for pardon. This act of reconciliation is a crucial part of the day’s observance, because G-d only forgives wrongdoings bein adam l'Makom, between people and G-d.

5. Wearing White

Many wear white clothing on Yom Kippur to symbolize purity and spiritual elevation. It is a reminder of the angelic state one aspires to achieve through repentance, as well as a reminder of the day of death.

6. The Closing Shofar Blast

The fast concludes with a single, long blast of the shofar, symbolizing the closing of the heavenly gates and sealing of the year’s judgment.

Through these mitzvot, Yom Kippur becomes a day of intense spiritual work, cleansing, and renewal, allowing each person to start the new year with a pure heart and a clear conscience.
