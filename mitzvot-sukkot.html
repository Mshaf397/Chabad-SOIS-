The Mitzvot of Sukkot

Sukkot, also known as the Festival of Booths, is a joyous holiday that commemorates the protection G-d provided the Jewish people in the wilderness. It is marked by several key mitzvot:

1. Dwelling in the Sukkah

The primary mitzvah of Sukkot is to dwell in a sukkah, a temporary hut covered with natural materials. For seven days, we eat, learn, and even sleep in the sukkah to remember G-d’s protection in the desert. Note that Chabad custom is to not sleep in the sukkah, although most Jews do.

2. Taking the Four Species

We are commanded to take the Four Species – the lulav (palm branch), etrog (citron), hadas (myrtle), and aravah (willow) – and shake them in six directions to acknowledge G-d’s presence everywhere.

3. Rejoicing in the Festival

Sukkot is a time of joy, referred to in the Torah as Zman Simchateinu, the “Time of Our Rejoicing.” The mitzvah is to celebrate by eating festive meals, singing, and being happy throughout the holiday.

4. The Ushpizin – Spiritual Guests

Each night of Sukkot, we welcome seven spiritual guests – Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, Joseph, and David – into the sukkah to inspire us and remind us of their virtues.

5. Hoshana Rabbah

The seventh day of Sukkot, known as Hoshana Rabbah, is a day of special prayers and rituals, including the beating of willow branches, symbolizing the culmination of the season’s judgment.

Sukkot is a time to embrace both the physical and spiritual, bringing together the temporary and the eternal through the mitzvot of the holiday.
