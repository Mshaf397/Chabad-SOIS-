The 39 Melachot, Forbidden Labors on Shabbat

In this article, we will discuss the 39 forbidden labors of Shabbat. We will also discuss common subcategories of these labors.

All primary categories of work (avos melachos, sing. av melacha) are derived from labors done in the Mishkan (Tabernacle).

1. Zorei'a / Planting

Planting, the av melachah of zorei'a, is forbidden on Shabbat, and includes any activity that promotes vegetative growth. One transgresses the melachah even if the planting produces no results; dropping a seed or pit on soil, for example. Since the seed could germinate and produce a tree or a plant, dropping the seed is considered an act of planting (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 336:10). It is important to note that we follow the halachic guidelines even when they seem obscure. For example, one may not place a potted plant onto grass on Shabbat, since the plant can now draw nutrients from the ground, improving its growth. Moreover, the plant is muktzeh, meaning it cannot be moved at all over Shabbat.

Source of the Labor:

Various herbs were planted to create the dyes used in the Mishkan (Rashi on Shabbat 73a). Additionally, wheat was planted to produce flour for some of the sacrifices and for the lechem hapanim, the showbread, which was baked and used in the Mishkan on a weekly basis. This is the opinion of Rabbi Hai Gaon.

Toladot (Subcategories) of Zorei'a include:

  • Watering grass (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 252:16)
  • Pruning trees
  • Weeding to improve growth of existing plants
  • Fumigating plants with pesticides.

Common activities to avoid:

  • Turning on a sprinkler system
  • Moving a plant to a place where it will get more sun
  • Dropping a pit or fruit seed on soil
  • Having a water fight on dirt or grass.

2. Choresh / Plowing

Plowing, the av melachah of choresh, is forbidden on Shabbat. When one plows, the plowshare cuts into the hard soil, loosening it, making it fertile ground in which to plant. Any other action which accomplishes this is included in the melachah of choresh. There is no minimum amount of soil required, since one can plant a seed even in a small area of earth that has been properly conditioned (Eglai Tal, Choresh 2). There is also no specific method in which the melachah must be done. Even scraping the ground with one’s shoe or a heavy chair—if it loosens the earth—is considered choresh.

Source of the Labor:

Plowing was part of the process to grow the herbs used to produce certain dyes for the Mishkan (Rashi on Shabbat 73a). Others source the melachah to the plowing used to produce wheat for some of the sacrifices and for the lechem hapanim, the showbread, which was baked on a weekly basis. This is the opinion of Rabbi Hai Gaon.

Toladot (Subcategories) of Choresh include:

  • Weeding plants (Maimonides, Mishneh Torah, Shabbat 8:1)
  • Pouring water on the ground to make it soft and suitable for planting (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 336:9)
  • Leveling the ground by filling in holes or removing pebbles (Mishneh Torah, Shabbat 21:2)
  • Applying soil enhancers and fertilizers (Chayei Adam, Shabbat 10:3).

Common activities to avoid:

  • Dragging a heavy chair along the ground
  • Raking the lawn
  • Scraping one's shoe against the ground.

3. Kotzeir / Reaping

Reaping, the av melachah of kotzeir, is forbidden on Shabbat and includes any activity which uses a tool to detach a plant, branch, or fruit from its source of growth. Inhibiting or preventing growth is not included. For example, neither taking flowers out of a vase nor shutting off a sprinkler system is included in kotzeir, even though both actions prevent further vegetative growth. Simply put, kotzeir has nothing to do with stopping something from growing; it is the simple act of severing a plant from its source of growth (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 336:19). The Jerusalem Talmud (Shabbat 48b, ch. 7 Halacha 2) understands that kotzeir can be applied to humans and animals as well. Halacha does not accept this definition in its final analysis, and although shearing wool and cutting nails are forbidden on Shabbat, they fall into the category of gozez (shearing), rather than kotzeir.

Source of the Labor:

Kotzair was done when they picked the herbs to make dyes for the Mishkan (Rashi on Shabbat 73a) and cut wheat to produce flour for some of the sacrifices and for the lechem hapanim, the showbread, which was baked in the Mishkan on a weekly basis. This is the opinion of Rabbi Hai Gaon.

Toladot (Subcategories) of Kotzeir include:

  • Lifting a perforated flower pot from the ground (because the plant in the pot is drawing sustenance from the ground) (Eglai Tal Kotzer 7)
  • Shaking a tree to cause the fruit to fall (ibid.) (because this is not the normal way of picking fruit, it only resembles the av melacha)
  • Picking a fruit or vegetable with one's hand (if it is normally picked with a tool) (Eglai Tal Kotzer 5).

Rabbinic safeguards against transgressing Kotzeir:

  • Making use of trees, including climbing, sitting, or leaning against a tree.
  • Smelling fruit which is still attached to the tree.
  • Eating fruit that fell from a tree on Shabbat.
  • Riding or using an animal on Shabbat (due to concern one might break off a branch to use as a prod).

Common activities to avoid:

  • Climbing trees
  • Picking flowers
  • Lying in a hammock or swing that is directly attached to a tree
  • Playing in a tree house
  • Picking up fruit that is lying on the ground under a tree.

4. Me'ameir / Gathering

Gathering produce, the av melachah of me’ameir, is forbidden on Shabbat (Eglai Tal, Me'amer 1, Shulchan Aruch Harav 340:15). In the Mishkan, me’ameir involved gathering produce in the field after it was cut. One only transgresses the biblical prohibition if he gathers produce which grew from the ground (Gidulei Karka) and is gathered at the location of growing (Makom Gidulo).

Source of the Labor:

The herbs and plants used in the dyes needed for the Mishkan were gathered after being cut (Rashi, Shabbat 73a). Additionally, the wheat for the flour used in some of the sacrifices and for the lechem hapanim, the showbread, was gathered after being harvested. This is the opinion of Rav Hai Gaon.

Toladot (Subcategories) of Me'ameir include:

  • Piercing holes into dates and threading them onto a string.
  • Making a fruit kebab out of whole, uncut fruits.
  • Pressing fruits together to form a solid mass (such as a ring of pressed figs).

Common activities to avoid:

  • Gathering sticks in the garden to form a pile (aside from the issue of muktzeh involved)
  • Collecting fruits that fell from a tree and putting them in a basket
  • Making a fruit kebab with whole, uncut fruit.

5. Dosh / Threshing

Threshing, the av melachah of dosh, is forbidden on Shabbat. Essentially, dosh is the removal of foods or liquids from where they are encased. The threshing in the Mishkan was done while the produce was still in the field. Most of our fruits and vegetables are peeled just before they are eaten, and the peels are considered part of the fruit, so peeling them is permitted. However, one should be careful not to use a peeler on Shabbat, and use a knife instead.

Source of the Labor:

Flour was needed for the lechem hapanim—the showbread which was baked on a weekly basis—and for some of the sacrifices. To produce the flour, the wheat needed to be threshed after it was harvested. Plants were also threshed to make dyes for parts of the Mishkan.

Mefarek, a common toladah of dosh (Mishneh Torah 21:12), includes:

  1. Removing peas or beans from an inedible pod.
  2. Milking a cow (Shabbat 95a; Mishneh Torah Hilchot Shabbat 8:7; Shulchan Aruch Harav 305:28).
  3. Squeezing grapes for juice and olives for oil (Shulchan Aruch HaRav 320:1, 3).
  4. Wringing liquids out of clothing or material.

Rabbinic enactments include:

  • Squeezing any fruit (Mishneh Torah 21:12, Shulchan Aruch HaRav 320:1, 3)
  • Defrosting liquids (Shabbat 51b; Mishneh Torah 21:13; Shulchan Aruch Harav 320:16).

Common activities to avoid:

  • Squeezing out a napkin when wiping up a spill
  • Squeezing lemon into a cup of tea
  • Squeezing out a sponge
  • Defrosting milk bottles.

*The content and structure of this article are based on the extensive halachic guides to the 39 Melachot available on Chabad.org.
